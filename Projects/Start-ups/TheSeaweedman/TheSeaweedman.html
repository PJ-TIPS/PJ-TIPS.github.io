The Seaweedman

An interview with Toine Wilke

Toine Wilke, a molecular biologist with a passion for seaweed is often referred to as 'The Seaweedman'. He has earned this nickname due to his mission; bringing seaweed from the ocean to your plate. Toine is the creator of the seaweed company Sea Green.



In this edited conversation with Peter Green, Toine spoke about Sea Green and where he sees the industry going.


What was the motivation behind getting involved with seaweed?


I noticed that our food system was unsustainable for the planet and our bodies. I felt like we could do so much better and one of the easiest ways to correct these things is to start eating seaweed.

Making seaweed a part of the diet could contribute to a healthier world and I find that motivating. One of the hardest parts of this mission is to make it attractive enough for ordinary people to actually try it. To make that first step is very difficult.


"Making seaweed a part of the diet could contribute to a healthier world "

What have been some of the challenges?

 

Getting people to try it in the right way has been a challenge. So has getting people to learn to appreciate seaweed.

We started Sea Green with seaweed burgers which was great. However, what I've come to know over the last half year is that we are not solving a real problem with a seaweed burger. We didn't have a problem-solution fit which is exacerbated by there being multiple other alternative proteins available. A seaweed burger is not really a good solution for people who want to eat more plant-based diets.

Seaweed is not that helpful as a meat alternative in veggie burgers. The Sea Green fish burgers are selling a whole lot better because people understand that fish is healthy, seaweed is healthy and both come from the sea. That's more of a problem solution fit.


What's your perspective on the future of seaweed?

 

We're still in Kindergarten - and that's what people told me five years ago! I think in the European food industry we want to use and develop two already existing seaweed products; Nori sheets, and Wakame. We can easily do that here. If we don't commit to doing seaweed in Europe, the industry will remain small. That's partly due to the government playing such a big role in giving out grants; they are effectively in charge of who gets the money and who doesn't.

We should definitely learn from Asia and get all the expertise from there. There's people working on that now.

 

What's next for Sea Green?


We're going to do a new crowdfunding campaign in September for some new products. We are using four different seaweeds in a new product release aimed to address problems relating to availability, how to use seaweed, why use seaweed and user-friendliness.


What advice might you give to your younger self or someone else early on in their entrepreneurial path?


Be sure about what problem you are actually solving. Make sure there's a fit between the problems you actually notice and the solutions that are out there.


"Be sure about what problem you are actually solving. "

Any books you might recommend?


I recently read the book by Eat like a fish by Bren Smith which I really enjoyed.
